We will start by stating a few assumptions:

  1. We will always start and end our strategy in USD - as we might assume that the investor of this strategy primarily uses USD.
  2. We will only use the set {THB, RON, JPY, IDR} as our possible lending and borrowing currencies. NB: although the JPY is the only developed currency and section 4.2 of the assignment mentions that "the borrow leg in mature-economy currencies such as JPY, USD, and EUR", we will not be using USD or EUR as the borrowing currencies in order to keep the trade simple and managable. As mentioned, we will report all PnL in USD.
  3. We assume we have 10MM USD, in total, 2MM of which we supply and 8MM of which is borrowed to use to trade for each potential currency pair. As such, losses midstrategy do not affect the total amount of capital available to invest. Although this is potentially unrealistic as an expectation, for the purposes of this assignment it will suffice.
  4. We will use FX rates from the day close on Wednesday. NB: FX markets trade 24/5 so in some sense, this is a more realistic assumption than assuming we can trade at end of day prices for US equities. However, it is still potentially unrealistic to assume we would get these exact prices, nevertheless, we are going to let this assumption hold.

Step 2: Designing the Algorithm¶

It is helpful now to review the strategy as a whole - in aggregate for week t and t+1:

Borrowing Side at t:

  • We borrow 8MM USD worth of the local currency

Borrowing Side at t+1:

  • We return our 8MM USD worth of the local currency plus the 3M + 50bps interest at the new exchange rate

Lending Side at t:

  • We buy a bond that will pay the 5YR rate as if it were a zero-coupon bond

Lending Side at t+1:

  • We sell the bond as if it were a zero-coupon bond and convert the proceeds to USD

def determine_pairs_to_trade(day):
    '''
    A helper function which takes in a day (a wednesday)
    as a datetime or string object. Then, determines which 
    pairs to trade, based on the condition that one currency's 5yr 
    rate is more than 50bps higher than the other's.
    '''
    out_lst = []
    
    #we get every 5yr rate 
    JPY_5yr = JPY.loc[day]['5-Year']
    IDR_5yr = IDR.loc[day]['5-Year']
    RON_5yr = RON.loc[day]['5-Year']
    THB_5yr = THB.loc[day]['5-Year']
    
    fiveyrs = { 'JPY' : JPY_5yr, 
               'IDR' : IDR_5yr, 
               'RON' : RON_5yr, 
               'THB' : THB_5yr}
    
    #we compare each currency to every other one 
    for currency, rate in fiveyrs.items():
        for currency_1, rate_1 in fiveyrs.items(): 
            #if its rate is greater than the other plus 50 bps then we will be
            #lending on this currency and borrowing on the other 
            if rate > rate_1 + 0.50:
                out_lst.append((currency, currency_1))
    return out_lst

    currencies = ['JPY', 'THB', 'IDR', 'RON']

                all_potential_pairs.append(c+'/'+c_1)

    for pair in all_potential_pairs:

def get_bond_values(rates_0, rates_1):
    '''
    A helper function which takes in rates_0 and rates_1
    these are series of the 1, 3, and 5yr rates at weeks
    t and t+1. We then figure out the prices of a bond 
    with 5yr coupons for t_0 and t_1 (week and next week). 
    From here, we find the zero-coupon bond value and then the 
    change between the two. 
    '''
    #to get present value of bond 
    #https://www.accaglobal.com/lk/en/student/exam-support-resources/professional-exams-study-resources/p4/technical-articles/bond-valuation-yields.html

    #face_value is not important for this calculation, just a place holder
    #we calculate the change over time which is independant of face_value
    face_value = 100
    
    one_yr_0 = rates_0[0] / 100
    three_yr_0 = rates_0[1] / 100
    five_yr_0 = rates_0[2] / 100

    one_yr_1 = rates_1[0] / 100 
    three_yr_1 = rates_1[1] / 100
    five_yr_1 = rates_1[2] / 100
    
    
    #coupons stay the same between both bonds! 
    coupons = np.ones(20) * face_value * five_yr_0 / 4
    coupons[-1] += face_value
    
    rate_0 = np.append(np.append(np.ones(6) * one_yr_0, np.ones(8) * three_yr_0) , np.ones(6) * five_yr_0)
    rate_1 = np.append(np.append(np.ones(6) * one_yr_1, np.ones(8) * three_yr_1) , np.ones(6) * five_yr_1)
    
    time_0 = np.arange(0.25, 5.25, 0.25)
    time_1 = time_0 - (1/52)
    
    #we now discount the value of the coupons based on the times 
    #and the yield curves 
    pv_coupon_0 = coupons/((1+rate_0)**time_0)
    pv_bond_0 = sum(pv_coupon_0)

    pv_coupon_1 = coupons/((1+rate_1)**time_1)
    pv_bond_1 = sum(pv_coupon_1)
    
    
    #in some cases, we have negative interest rates, 
    #this is to handle those instances
    if sum(coupons) >= 0: 
        yld_0 = (sum(coupons)/pv_bond_0)**(1/5) - 1
        yld_1 = (sum(coupons)/pv_bond_1)**(1/(5-(1/52))) - 1
    if sum(coupons) < 0: 
        yld_0 = ((-1*sum(coupons)) / pv_bond_0)**(1/5) - 1 
        yld_0 = -1 * yld_0
        
        yld_1 = ((-1*sum(coupons)) / pv_bond_1)**(1/(5-(1/52))) - 1
        yld_1 = -1 * yld_1
    

    #now get the zero_coupon_bond price 
    #https://www.investopedia.com/terms/z/zero-couponbond.asp
    zero_coupon_price_0 = face_value / (1+yld_0)**5
    zero_coupon_price_1 = face_value / (1+yld_1)**(5 - 1/52)

    #get the change from the zero coupon price from t=0 to t=1 
    pct_change = (zero_coupon_price_1 - zero_coupon_price_0) / zero_coupon_price_0
    
    return pct_change

Although there is a lot of data to see above, it seems that we are using THB as our borrowing currency for two trades RON/THB and IDR/RON during the 2017 to 2018 period, thus the dip that we saw above makes a good deal of sense. We also notice that the vast majority of the time, we are using the same pairs to trade, this makes sense given the fact that the differences between them fluctuate but for the most part stay above or below the 50 bps apart threshold. Only in one case over the period (RON/THB) does it happen that we both enter and exit the trade. If we were doing this in real life, and transaction costs were a concern, we might consider limiting ourselves to only the trades where we don't have to enter and exit often (everything except RON/THB).

Further, we can look at the distribution of our borrowing costs to see something interesting below. We have that the skew is very low and that the kurtosis is also negative just about one. This tells us that the tail of the distribution is longer towards the right-hand side of the curve (i.e. lower costs) and kurtosis being low tells us that the data has a lack of outliers and is light-tailed. I suppose that this is good for us because we know that we have relatively the same costs we sometimes the costs shifting a bit lower than average. This makes sense because over the period we saw moments where the costs moved more sharply down and take longer to go back up. Economically, (potentially) this means that when central banks raise their rates (at least for these currencies) they cut them slowly and raise them more quickly, otherwise, it implies that rates jump faster than they fall. This is an interesting finding and potentially rather counterintuitive given that at least in equities, things tend to rise slowly and then fall quickly. I suppose that inflationary expectations, a factor that influences interest rates, might have the fear mentality that falling equities share. These expectations might cause central banks and markets to raise rates quickly.

A couple of thoughts on portfolio returns: We see that our skew of weekly returns was -0.3 and kurtosis was about 11 while the skew of annual returns was about -0.4 with kurtosis of 0.20. This makes a good deal of sense given that usually, returns on a weekly basis will have a much wider range than returns on an annual basis. We only had one major drawdown in in weekly performance in 2020. If we look at the appendix section (Market Conditions), we see that many currencies experienced wide changes in their 5 year rates as well as their FX rates, this uncertainty in the markets caused our portfolio to move sharply against us.

The kurtosis for the annual returns being very low is a great sign that we have few outliers, the kurtosis for the weekly returns being so high is not great but to be expected. There was only one period of prolonged drawdown from 2018 into 2019, and even so, the moves were not very bad.

A couple of concluding ideas:

  1. This strategy showed remarkably good performance, in essence, a carry trade is an insurance policy - we're picking up pennies in front of a steamroller. It seems that perhaps, we might have missed the worst moves although as we can see in market conditions, there were a variety of times when our portfolio drawback was correlated with market moves in FX rates or interest rates.
  2. One factor which definitely helped our stellar performance was the high leverage we were able to access. Assuming that we are a large enough fund, it is not unreasonable to think we could access such capital.
  3. We completely ignored transaction costs. Given that the strategy assumes that we rebalance every week by selling and buying everything again, transaction costs would have definitely hurt our performance. However, in practice, since we did not really change the positions we were taking, it is possible that we could have keep transaction costs extremely low.
  4. The window we tested on, compared to the frequency of geopolitical or other events that affect FX markets and interest rate markets was rather small. We were constrained due to the data, however, if we tested this strategy over a different or larger window, it seems likely that our results could be very different.
  5. It doesn't seem like there was a regime change at all during our testing window. Our performance was much more varied earlier on, weekly returns much more spread out, however, as time went on, for the most part, our returns became slightly more stable. If there were a regime change in the market, we might expect to see the market move or trade differently i.e. the variance of the weekly returns would move a lot or the average weekly returns would converge around 0 if many people entered. If you look at the rolling 26 week mean of weekly returns, we see that it continues to vary which implies that it is unlikely that the market players meaningfully changed during the period.
